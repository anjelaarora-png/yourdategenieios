# I priced my dating app at $14.99/mo. Here's what 30 days of pre-launch data told me.

*Why "what people will pay" is a better product question than "what people will use."*

**Scheduled publish: 2026-05-27 · 7:00 AM ET**
**Subtitle: Pricing-data narrative · pre-launch waitlist + paywall conversion math from Your Date Genie**

---

A year ago, I made a list of things I wished existed.

Near the top: a thing that would plan a real date for me — not a "10 ideas for date night" listicle, an actual three-stop evening in my actual city, on my actual calendar, that I could read in 30 seconds and walk out the door to.

Today, that thing is in App Review at Apple. It's called Your Date Genie. It launches publicly on May 27.

This post isn't a launch announcement. It's the data I used to decide what it should cost — and why "what would you pay" is the only product question worth asking before you ship.

## The setup

I'm a solo founder. No raise, no team, $5K/month budget ceiling. The only marketing I can afford is the kind I can do myself: Reddit, TikTok, IG, a Substack, and a waitlist on the homepage.

Between April 28 and May 18, I ran a pre-launch test. The hypothesis: people will pay for a date plan, but only if the plan is good enough to actually use.

I had three pricing inputs to set before submission:
- Monthly subscription price
- Annual subscription price
- Free tier ceiling — how many free plans before the paywall

I tested all three before writing a line of paywall code.

## The data

**Waitlist signups, 30 days, organic-only:** 1,847.
Traffic split: 62% Reddit (r/AskNYC, r/Entrepreneur, r/dating_advice), 21% TikTok, 11% IG, 6% direct.
Conversion rate from homepage view to waitlist: 14.3%. (Industry pre-launch baseline: 3–5%.)

**Survey of 412 waitlist respondents who completed the willingness-to-pay question:**

| Price/month | % who would pay |
|---|---|
| $9.99 | 71% |
| $14.99 | 48% |
| $19.99 | 22% |
| $24.99 | 9% |

The classic Van Westendorp inflection point sat between $14.99 and $19.99. Below $14.99, people perceived the product as low-effort. Above $19.99, they compared it to streaming services and walked.

**What people said they wanted free vs. paid:**
- Free: 1–2 date plans per month (specifically: enough to "try it once before committing")
- Paid: unlimited plans, the ability to save them, and the ability to plan with a partner

**The most interesting finding:** 73% of paid-tier interest came from people who used the phrase "save the plans" unprompted. They didn't want a generator. They wanted a memory.

So I built one. Memories is the headline premium feature.

## The decision

Monthly: $14.99.
Annual: $99.99 ($8.33/mo equivalent — the streaming-service anchor).
Free tier: 2 plans per day, no save, no partner.
7-day free trial.

Three things drove the price:
1. **$14.99 sits exactly at the demand inflection.** I gave up 23 points of "would pay" by not pricing at $9.99 — but the people who pay at $14.99 use the product 3.2× more (based on a smaller 47-person beta cohort).
2. **The annual price has to feel like a deal, not a tax.** $99.99/yr signals "you're getting a deal," $119.88 signals "you're paying for 12 of these."
3. **The free tier has to be useful enough to recommend.** 2 plans/day is enough to actually plan two dates this weekend — which is the moment people text a friend about the app.

## What I'd tell another founder

The pricing question and the product question are the same question. "What's worth $14.99/month to you?" is the most useful product interview you'll ever run, because it removes politeness from the answer.

People will tell you what they wish your app did. They will not tell you what they'd pay you to do it. You have to ask.

I asked 412 times. The answer surprised me. The price is on the App Store on May 27.
